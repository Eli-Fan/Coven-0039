Human Defined City

Chapter 1 — Public

Public

The public body stands as an object before the city. Framed by walls already marked and overwritten, it becomes another surface among surfaces. Expression flattens under exposure. The face does not declare emotion; it absorbs projection. Placed against pavement, the body appears almost architectural — aligned to the edge of concrete, measured by curb and brick. In public space, stillness reads as spectacle. The city does not acknowledge the figure; it merely situates it. Presence becomes temporary installation. A neck, a spine, a step across printed surfaces — each movement leaves a trace, but none are permanent. Walls accumulate inscriptions; bodies accumulate proximity. The two begin to resemble each other.

Public space invites inscription. The spray gesture fractures the illusion of passive observation. Marking is not decorative; it is an act of negotiation with space. To write on the city is to momentarily reclaim it. Yet the mark, like the body, will eventually fade into repetition. In the public sphere, resistance and spectacle collapse into the same surface.

Chapter 2 — Structure

Structure

The body becomes an interruption that fails to interrupt. Passengers board, screens flicker, trains arrive. Structure absorbs contrast without acknowledging it. Even theatrical presence is reduced to movement along a designated path. Infrastructure does not oppose individuality; it neutralizes it through repetition.

The figure becomes an object within architecture, marked and segmented as space itself is segmented. Reflections in mirrors and corners fracture the image into angles. Surveillance becomes perspective; perspective becomes control. Within structure, identity is not erased — it is organized. The city does not remove the human; it rearranges them into form.

Chapter 3 — Resident

Resident

The resident is not monumental. She stands against peeling walls, bus stops, cracked pavement — places designed for transit, not permanence. The bright gloves and stockings appear almost utilitarian, almost protective, yet strangely ornamental. Survival becomes aesthetic. The phone glows in the hand like a private territory within public decay. Between storefront reflections and empty waiting benches, presence feels provisional. The resident occupies space without claiming it. The city does not announce her existence; it simply continues around her.

Here the resident merges with the margins.Garbage bags stack like silent architecture. The white tulle skirt, fragile and ceremonial, contrasts with the heaviness of discarded matter. Clean and waste coexist without hierarchy. The hood conceals expression, but the gaze remains steady. To live within the city is to inhabit its leftovers — economic, emotional, material. The resident is not alien to the structure; she is its byproduct. In the quiet accumulation of debris, identity becomes something worn lightly, almost defensively, against collapse.
